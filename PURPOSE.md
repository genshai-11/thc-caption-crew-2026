# Purpose — Caption & Crew (THC)

## Why this project exists
Caption & Crew exists to explore a simple but important language-learning question:

**Can one person hear a spoken idea in one language and quickly deliver the same meaning in another language under time pressure?**

This project is not mainly about literal translation.
It is not mainly about grammar perfection.
It is not mainly about pronunciation scoring.

Its core purpose is to measure and train **meaning transfer through speech**.

---

## The central idea
There are two players:
- **Captain** creates the source meaning in Vietnamese
- **Crew** must capture and carry that meaning into English

The product turns this into a compact game loop:
- hear
- understand
- respond
- compare meaning

This makes speaking practice feel more alive, more social, and more cognitively meaningful than isolated repetition.

---

## What the project is trying to prove
Caption & Crew is trying to prove that a speaking game can be built around:
1. **meaning preservation**, not word matching
2. **fast response under pressure**, not only careful homework-style answers
3. **two-player interaction**, not solo drilling
4. **speech-first gameplay**, not text-first UI

---

## Product philosophy

### 1. Meaning over wording
A response can be good even if it does not use the same words.
If the intended meaning is preserved, the system should recognize that.

### 2. Speech over text
Players should speak, listen, and react.
Text exists mainly for system processing and admin validation, not as the center of the player experience.

### 3. Minimal over cluttered
The game should feel calm, immediate, and intuitive.
Every extra label, card, border, and transcript competes with the actual human interaction.

### 4. Privacy during play
During the round, the system should not reveal transcripts that could spoil the task.
The game must protect the integrity of the challenge.

### 5. Analysis after action
The best moment to analyze is after both players have finished speaking.
The product should let people play first, then compute the meaning result.

---

## What makes this product different
Many speaking tools ask:
- Did you pronounce it correctly?
- Did you say the expected sentence?
- Did you type the right translation?

Caption & Crew asks something more human:
- **Did you truly understand the meaning?**
- **Could you respond quickly?**
- **Could you carry the idea across languages?**

That is the heart of the product.

---

## Intended feeling
The product should feel like:
- a speaking challenge
- a role-based interaction
- a focused language game
- a modern minimal mobile experience

It should **not** feel like:
- an AI dashboard
- a debugging tool
- a transcript viewer
- a form-heavy educational admin panel

---

## Role of AI in this project
AI is not the product itself.
AI is the invisible engine behind the product.

AI helps with:
- transcribing speech
- comparing meaning
- generating compact feedback
- letting admins tune the evaluation behavior

But the product is fundamentally about **human communication**, not showing off AI features.

---

## Admin purpose
The admin layer exists to make experimentation possible.
It allows the builder or teacher to:
- configure providers
- test readiness
- tune how meaning is judged
- control how much feedback should appear

Admin features are important, but they are in service of the game, not the center of it.

---

## Long-term direction
In the long term, Caption & Crew can grow into:
- a classroom speaking activity
- a pair-learning tool
- a meaning-transfer training system
- a coach mode with optional feedback depth
- a broader family of speech-based learning games

But the core should remain simple:

**One person speaks. Another person responds. The system checks whether the meaning survived.**

---

## Decision filter for future changes
When adding new features, ask:
1. Does this help players understand and transfer meaning better?
2. Does this keep the gameplay fast and clear?
3. Does this avoid leaking information during play?
4. Does this preserve the minimal, touch-first experience?
5. Is this helping the core game, or turning it into a dashboard?

If a feature fails these questions, it likely does not belong in the core experience.
